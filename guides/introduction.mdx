ProductBrain synthesises two established frameworks into a single bi-modal visualization. The same dataset renders as either a **Planning Tree** (strategy) or a **Delivery Map** (execution), with animated transitions between them.

## The Problem

Anyone who has ever defended a roadmap to a stakeholder knows the feeling: the logic is in your head, but it's not traceable. You know why you're building this feature instead of that one, but you can't show the chain of reasoning. When priorities shift, nobody can see the cascade of impact.

Meanwhile, the tools you have don't help:
- **Jira** tracks tasks, not strategy. It knows what you're doing but not why.
- **Miro** captures ideas, not structure. Anything goes — which means nothing is forced.
- **Slide decks** communicate a point-in-time snapshot that's stale by the time you present it.

ProductBrain makes the logic **traceable and always current**. Every piece of work traces through a chain — job → approach → need → goal — that answers "why are we building this?" for anyone who asks. When you add a third approach under a need, you see the other two get relatively smaller. When you tag jobs to an iteration, you see what's *not* in the iteration. The tradeoffs are visible by default.

## Two Views, One Dataset

  <Card title="Planning Tree" icon="diagram-project">
    **Goal → Need → Approach → Job.** The strategic view. Trace any piece of work back to the business outcome it serves. See what you're betting on and why.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Delivery Map" icon="table-columns">
    **Approaches × Iterations.** The execution view. Bundle work into releases. See what ships when, and what proof you need that each bet is paying off.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

These aren't separate tools — they're two lenses on the same data. The Planning Tree is where you think. The Delivery Map is where you ship. Toggle between them at any time. The person setting the agenda works in the tree. The person (or agent) delivering the work reads the map.
